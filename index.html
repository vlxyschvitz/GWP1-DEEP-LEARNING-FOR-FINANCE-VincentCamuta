Step 7

Comparative Analysis & Discussion

Group synthesis of model performance, architecture trade-offs, and practical implications for asset allocation.

7.1 Architecture comparison: MLP vs CNN-GAF vs LSTM

The three architectures differ fundamentally in how they encode temporal information. The MLP treats the 60-day return window as a flat feature vector, so it can identify patterns associated with specific lag positions but cannot capture sequential order or local temporal coherence. The CNN-GAF first projects the return series into a 2D angular-field image, allowing convolutional filters to detect local co-movement patterns regardless of exact lag position — essentially acting as a bank of trainable correlation detectors. The LSTM, by contrast, processes the sequence step by step through gated memory cells, naturally encoding long-range dependencies and regime persistence.

  • Equity (SPY): CNN-GAF leads, likely because short-term momentum clusters appear as coherent image patterns that convolutional filters learn efficiently.
  • Long Bonds (TLT): LSTM leads, consistent with interest-rate regimes that persist over months and create autocorrelated return dynamics well-suited to recurrent memory.
  • Short Bonds (SHY): All three perform similarly — the near-random, low-volatility character of short-duration yields limits predictability regardless of architecture.
  • Gold (GLD): LSTM leads marginally; gold returns exhibit mild mean-reversion within trending regimes, favouring sequential processing.
  • Oil (DBO): All models struggle; oil is dominated by supply shocks that create non-stationary regime breaks that no architecture trained on historical patterns can anticipate.

7.2 Multi-output vs single-output LSTM

The multi-output model shares representations across assets through the concatenated feature layer and the two shared dense layers that follow. This creates an implicit regularisation channel: when cross-asset correlations are informative, the shared weights propagate gradient signals from one asset's prediction error to another's branch — something single-output models cannot do by construction.

Empirically, the multi-output model improves directional accuracy on Long Bonds and Gold — precisely the assets whose returns are most strongly correlated with Equity (via the equity–duration channel and risk-off dynamics). Short Bonds and Oil, being the least correlated with the system, show negligible improvement, supporting the hypothesis that the gain is specifically from cross-asset signal sharing rather than architecture complexity alone.

7.3 Backtesting results vs equally-weighted benchmark

The long–short strategy exploiting the multi-output LSTM's rankings achieves a higher Sharpe ratio than the equally-weighted benchmark over 2021–2022 in most configurations. However, the absolute total return advantage narrows when 2022's inflationary environment is included, as the high cross-asset correlation during rate-hike periods reduces the diversification value of the long–short construction.

The hit rate — the fraction of 25-day periods where the strategy earns a positive return — is above 50%, confirming that the directional accuracy translates into practical alpha. The maximum drawdown is lower than the benchmark, consistent with the hedging benefit of short positions in underperforming assets. These results suggest the multi-asset deep learning framework provides genuine tactical value, subject to the caveat that the 2021–2022 test window is a single macroeconomic regime and out-of-sample robustness across regimes remains to be tested.
